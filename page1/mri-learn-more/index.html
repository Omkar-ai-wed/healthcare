How MRI Works

Understanding the technology behind magnetic resonance imaging

1

Magnetic Field Alignment

Strong magnetic field (1.5-3 Tesla) aligns hydrogen atoms (protons) in your body

The MRI scanner creates a powerful magnetic field thousands of times stronger than Earth's magnetic field

2

Radio Wave Pulse

Radio waves are pulsed through the body, disturbing the alignment of protons

These radio frequency pulses are specific to hydrogen atoms and cause them to absorb energy

3

Signal Detection

As protons return to alignment, they release energy that is detected by receiver coils

Different tissues release energy at different rates, creating tissue-specific signals

4

Image Reconstruction

Computer processes signals and converts them into detailed cross-sectional images

Advanced algorithms create high-resolution images showing anatomy and pathology

Medical Applications

Comprehensive overview of MRI uses across medical specialties

Brain & Spine Imaging

Exceptional detail of brain tissue, white matter, and spinal cord structures

Common Applications:

  • Stroke diagnosis and monitoring
  • Brain tumors and masses
  • Multiple sclerosis
  • Epilepsy evaluation
  • Dementia and memory disorders
  • Spinal cord injuries
  • Herniated discs
  • Spinal stenosis

Joints & Soft Tissues

Superior visualization of cartilage, ligaments, and soft tissues not visible on X-rays

Common Applications:

  • Sports injuries (ACL, rotator cuff)
  • Joint cartilage evaluation
  • Ligament and tendon tears
  • Soft tissue masses
  • Arthritis assessment
  • Bone infections
  • Muscle disorders
  • Joint replacement evaluation

Abdomen & Pelvis

Excellent soft tissue contrast without radiation, ideal for serial monitoring

Common Applications:

  • Liver tumors and cirrhosis
  • Kidney masses and stones
  • Pancreatic disorders
  • Uterine fibroids
  • Ovarian cysts
  • Prostate evaluation
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Adrenal gland disorders

Heart & Blood Vessels

Comprehensive cardiac function assessment without radiation or invasive procedures

Common Applications:

  • Heart muscle damage assessment
  • Congenital heart disease
  • Aortic aneurysms
  • Heart valve function
  • Cardiomyopathy
  • Cardiac masses
  • Pericardial disease
  • Vascular malformations

Breast Imaging

Most sensitive method for breast cancer detection, especially in dense tissue

Common Applications:

  • High-risk screening (BRCA carriers)
  • Dense breast tissue evaluation
  • Cancer extent assessment
  • Treatment response monitoring
  • Implant evaluation
  • Silicone leak detection
  • Occult primary cancer
  • Pre-surgical planning

When Doctors Choose MRI

Brain & Nervous System

MRI is preferred for detailed brain imaging, stroke evaluation, and neurological conditions due to superior soft tissue contrast.

Joint & Muscle Problems

Essential for evaluating cartilage, ligaments, and soft tissue injuries that X-rays cannot detect.

Repeated Monitoring

Safe for frequent follow-up scans due to no radiation exposure, ideal for chronic conditions.

Pediatric Imaging

Preferred for children when possible to avoid radiation exposure during development.

Types of MRI Exams

Understanding different MRI sequences and specialized techniques

T1-Weighted

Description: Provides excellent anatomical detail

Appearance: Fat appears bright, fluid appears dark

Uses: Anatomy, post-contrast enhancement

T2-Weighted

Description: Shows pathology and inflammation well

Appearance: Fluid appears bright, fat appears intermediate

Uses: Detecting edema, tumors, inflammation

FLAIR

Description: Suppresses normal fluid signals

Appearance: Highlights abnormal areas near fluid

Uses: Brain lesions, multiple sclerosis

Diffusion (DWI)

Description: Detects restricted water movement

Appearance: Acute strokes appear bright

Uses: Stroke, infections, some tumors

MR Angiography (MRA)

Purpose: Images blood vessels without invasive procedures

Applications: Aneurysms, stenosis, vascular malformations

Functional MRI (fMRI)

Purpose: Maps brain activity during tasks

Applications: Pre-surgical brain mapping, research

Cardiac MRI

Purpose: Comprehensive heart structure and function

Applications: Heart disease, function assessment

MRCP

Purpose: Images bile ducts and pancreatic ducts

Applications: Gallstones, duct obstruction, pancreatitis

Gadolinium-Based Contrast Agents

MRI contrast agents help enhance the visibility of certain tissues and abnormalities.

Advantages

  • Much safer than iodine-based CT contrast
  • Less than 0.1% severe reaction rate
  • Better tumor detection and characterization
  • Vascular imaging enhancement

Considerations

  • Caution in severe kidney disease (eGFR <30)
  • Generally avoided in pregnancy
  • May cause temporary metallic taste
  • Kidney function testing may be required

Understanding Common Terms

Normal vs. Abnormal

+

"Normal" means no significant abnormalities were detected. "Abnormal" doesn't always mean serious - it could indicate minor changes that need monitoring or are age-related.

Enhancement

+

Areas that appear brighter after contrast injection. This can help identify inflammation, tumors, or increased blood flow to tissues.

Signal Intensity

+

Describes how bright or dark tissues appear on MRI images. Different signal intensities help identify various tissue types and abnormalities.

Incidental Findings

+

Unexpected findings unrelated to the reason for your MRI. Most are benign but may require follow-up to ensure they remain stable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions and concerns about MRI procedures

Safety & General Questions

Is MRI safe?

+

MRI is very safe with no ionizing radiation. The magnetic fields and radio waves used have no known harmful effects. Millions of MRI scans are performed safely each year.

What if I'm claustrophobic?

+

Many options are available including open-bore scanners, sedation, music, and support from our staff. Most patients complete their exam successfully even with claustrophobia.

Can I have an MRI with metal implants?

+

Many implants are MRI-safe, especially newer ones. We'll review your specific implants and may need documentation from your doctor or the device manufacturer.

Procedure Questions

How long does an MRI take?

+

Most MRI exams take 30-90 minutes, depending on the body part being imaged and the number of sequences needed. You'll know the expected time before your exam.

Do I need contrast for my MRI?

+

Contrast is used in about 30% of MRI exams to enhance certain structures or detect specific conditions. Your doctor will determine if contrast is needed for your particular study.

Can I eat before my MRI?

+

For most MRI exams, you can eat normally. However, some abdominal studies may require fasting. You'll receive specific instructions when you schedule your exam.
